American Robin (Turdus migratorius)

 

Photo1. American Robin - Karney, Lee USFWS (full credits below)

Description:   This member of the thrush family is 9 to 10 inches long and weighs about 3 ounces. Males are gray-brown with round reddish breasts, dark heads, and white crescents above and below each eye. Females and juveniles are are similar but lighter in color. Juveniles have dark brown spots on the chest.

 

Migration & Habitat:  Robins can tolerate cold temperatures thus do not migrate unless forced by food scarcity and poor weather. They typically migrate to the Southwest, Mexico, and the Gulf Coast.

Range map from, © Arizona Board of Regents/ASU Ask A Biologist. (Full credits below)

 

Reproduction:   Robins build a nest in trees or shrubs with dried fibers, grass, twigs, roots, and mud. The female lays from 3 to 5 eggs per clutch and may produce 1 to 3 broods each year. The eggs hatch after about 2 weeks and the young leave the nest in 14 to 16 days.

 

Diet:  Their diet is primarily invertebrates (such as beetle grubs, earthworms, and caterpillars), fruits, and berries.

 

Conservation Status:  Robins in Oregon are most abundant in the Willamette Valley and from Lincon County northward. Populations have slightly increased, and they are a low conservation concern. Ground foraging makes them vulnerable to pesticide poisoning and predation by cats.

 

Fun Facts:

  • Robins are fast fliers, up to 20-35 miles per hour in good weather.
  • They live about 2 years in the wild. Some live 6 to 9 years and some lived almost 14 years.
  • Their keen eyesight can see tiny disturbances in the soil that indicate food.
  • Robins pair up in the spring but go separate ways once their family is raised.
  • Records show robins once migrated from Iowa to Alaska (about 3,000 miles).
